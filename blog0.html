The Unpredictable Weather: Journey From Determinism to Chaos

Anubhab Mukherjee | 02 June 2026

In the morning news, it was forecasted that it would be a sunny day. Hearing that you planned for a trip with your friends. But in the middle of your journey a torrential rain lashed out all your hope. Naturally all your grievances would fall on the news channel and on those meteorologists who you think are the most incompetent.

This is a very familiar scenario for the 20th and early 21st century people, who went through these mendacious weather reports. Today the situation has much improved. Now we can predict the weather for upcoming 15 days with good precision, albeit in most of the cases you can still see a drastic change in the weather data for the days 10 to 15, if you check it daily. Thus, the struggle is ongoing. I know, it's very obvious to raise the point that while scientists are advancing in the new era of artificial intelligence, we are still struggling with predicting the weather. Why so? Why does this 15-day barrier still exist? Is it the incompetence of our meteorologists? Or, lack of knowledge in weather study? To find a precise answer to these queries we need to go back to the Newtonian time, when we had just come to know that it is the same universal force, “Gravitation”, which was governing the fall of an apple from a tree, along with the planetary motions.

Newton’s Deterministic Universe: The Ultimate Predictability

Before the discovery of gravitational force by Newton, the study of planetary motions was completely based on Astronomical observations. For instance, Kepler’s laws of planetary motions came from the comprehensive empirical observations of planets’ position in the night sky. It tells us about the geometry of the planet's trajectory around the Sun, but doesn’t explain why planets follow such laws. Newton was the first to address the “why”, using his theory of gravitation. His laws (including Newton’s laws of motion) were so powerful that they could predict the trajectories of planets with pin point accuracy. Basically, if we know the initial conditions of a system, more specifically the initial position and momentum, laws of mechanics can predict the future state of that system accurately with no ambiguity. Most of the contemporary physicists and philosophers were persuaded by this idea of determinism.

The universe, as illustrated by Newton, was itself a sui generis idea of determinism and predictability that governed the backbone of philosophical thoughts for centuries in the Western world. If we have the initial conditions of every particle of the universe, then the laws of physics allow us to predict the future of every individual particle, if not the entire future state of the Universe. This deterministic nature of the future inculcated the idea of ultimate predictability and control over humanity's unforeseen fortune. It made the idea of determinism and predictability equal, which served as a pivotal idea of Classical Physics until it was shaken by a seemingly simple problem from the Celestial mechanics, “the three body problem”.

Three Body Problem and Poincare's Approach

When Newton tried to synthesise his laws with Kepler’s, he approached the problem by taking the pair of Sun and planets one by one, more specifically the Sun and Earth as one pair. This is called a “two body problem”. The solution and physics both are simple and straight forward for this system. But the problem emerged when he added another celestial object in his calculation, for instance Sun, Earth and Moon, a “three body problem”. Unlike the two body problem, here the addition of a third object made the system non-linear in nature, which introduced immense complexity to the solution with multiple differential equations and constantly changing gravitational forces. This opened up an unforeseen fissure in the idea of relating determinism with complete predictability, which compelled the contemporary physicists and mathematicians to stamp the problem as “unsolvable”. But, the term “unsolvable” itself is very perplexing, we need a contemplative explanation on it.

The problem was not actually “unsolvable” in the physical sense, rather it was “unsolvable” for the scarcity of mathematical knowledge or analytical methods. In those days, physicists had been using analytical techniques to solve differential equations for the physical systems. But those techniques or methods were not capable enough to deal with the “three body problem”. They needed new ideas, new mathematical methods. Actually the worm in the apple was not the physical laws, it was the inherent property of the system that made the problem seemingly “unsolvable”. The system itself is deterministic, but predicting the long term future with precise accuracy is not possible. Why? The answer was given by Henri Poincare in his memoir, entitled “On the problem of the three bodies and the equations of dynamics”, that won the King Oscar prize in the year 1889. He approached the problem differently. Rather than solving the problem with conventional techniques, he asked the question whether the system is itself stable or not. In his memoir he showed that our solar system is not as stable as we think. The dependency of the system on initial conditions was highly sensitive and a subtle change in the condition can cause a drastic effect on the future state of the system. That means a single long term solution for the future state is not possible, though the system is deterministic. This idea directly contradicts Newton's predictable universe, where a minute change in initial state only affects the future minutely. Poincare’s approach planted the seed of a new branch in science, named “Chaos”. Chaos means an utter confusion or disorder and chaotic systems are highly sensitive to the initial conditions. It means our Solar system, or the three body problem is deterministic but the system itself is chaotic, that makes its long term future unpredictable. Now these chaotic systems are gonna play a crucial role in understanding the weather.

Lorenz’s Butterfly Effect and The Unpredictable Weather

Poincare’s notion for the chaotic systems lay dormant for half a century as there were no directly observable real life incidents that caught the eyes of scientists. But serendipitously a meteorologist in MIT rediscovered the plant of Poincare that linked chaos with weather. In the early 1960s, a few Hurricanes devastated the USA and all the blame went to the Weather Department and meteorologists. Edward Lorenz was one of those meteorologists in MIT, who was working with some computer models to simulate the weather patterns. Basically he was solving a bunch of differential equations with some real weather parameters and initial conditions gathered by weather balloons and satellites. One day, when he was busy with such models, he unexpectedly discovered chaos. Actually working with real weather data, during rerun of a model, he rounded the values of initial conditions. That means instead of putting the value 0.506127, he put 0.506 to save time. In Newtonian mechanics this minute change doesn’t affect the solution. But when Lorenz rerun the model with 0.506 value in the computer, it gave an utterly different output from what he got for the initial value of 0.506127. In the beginning he assumed that the error was coming because of a faulty computer system. But when he observed the outputs conscientiously, he found an astonishing result. At first, the difference between the outputs (divergence of the solutions or trajectories for two slightly different initial values, 0.506127 and 0.506) were subtle, but it grew exponentially into a large error within days. It stirred up Poincare's decades-old theory. From the outputs Lorenz was convinced that he was dealing with a chaotic system and felt the need of measuring initial conditions more precisely. So, for every minutely different initial values (0.12786, 0.12785, 0.12781 and so on) the weather model will show different weather results in the long run. This weather phenomenon was later named as ‘Butterfly Effect’, inspired from the title of a presentation given by Lorenz in MIT in the year 1972, “Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly's Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?”. Obviously this is a metaphor, but he deliberately used the title to apprise the point that how a little change in the past can make the future completely dicey.

Lorenz’s Butterfly Effect worked as an adjunct to connect Poincare’s mathematical theory with real life chaotic phenomenon. It urges the meteorologists to gather and use weather data more precisely. Nowadays we are taking help from the chain of satellites, weather balloons to amass more accurate data. We use multiple weather models as an ensemble to get a more precise weather forecast. But we have limits on how accurately we can measure. So, errors will be there no matter how hard we try to make our weather systems accurate. This is the beauty of nature, a universe that can be defined with simple, deterministic mathematical models, can still be unpredictable.

So, next time when you face such deceitful weather reports, don’t blame the meteorologists. Rather acknowledge the ultimate reality that instills the inherent ambiguity in nature that creates adversity in predicting the future.
